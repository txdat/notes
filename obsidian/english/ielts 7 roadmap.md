# IELTS 7.0 Roadmap — no band below 6.5 (for a busy software engineer)

> **12 months · ~2.5 hrs/day · ~900 hrs** | starting point ≈ A2/low-B1 → target ≈ C1
> Default: **Academic** module. General Training differences are flagged with **[GT]**.
> **Target: overall 7.0, with every band ≥ 6.5.**
> The overall is the average of the four bands rounded to the nearest 0.5. The safe plan is **Listening & Reading at 7.5, Writing & Speaking at 6.5** — that averages to **exactly 7.0** (7.5+7.5+6.5+6.5 = 28/4), so you hit target even if the productive skills only reach their 6.5 floor and nothing rounds in your favor. Listening and Reading rise fastest and are the most controllable, so **make them your buffer**.
> Band → raw score: Listening 7.5 ≈ **32/40**, Reading (Academic) 7.5 ≈ **33/40**. Aim **34+** for margin.

---

## Reality Check (read this first)

- This is **aggressive** — "very limited" to a clean 7 (no band under 6.5) in a year is a real stretch, and it works *only* with daily consistency. The enemy is skipped days, not slow days. If you fall behind, the honest move is to push the test date, not lower the bar.
- The **"no band below 6.5" rule is the hard part.** A single weak skill sinks you even if the others are strong. So there is **no coasting on any section** — your weakest skill, not your average, decides success. Track all four every week and always feed the lagging one.
- The band you get is decided mostly by **vocabulary size + grammatical accuracy + hours of input**, not by "IELTS tricks." Spend Months 1–6 building the language; spend Months 7–12 aiming it at the exam. Do **not** start doing full IELTS tests in Month 1 — you'll just memorize wrong habits.
- Your two hardest sections will be **Writing** and **Speaking** (productive skills) — and these are exactly where the 6.5 floor bites. Getting Writing from a 6 to a reliable 6.5 is the single biggest project of the year; budget the most feedback there. Listening and Reading rise faster and are more controllable (your engineering reading habit transfers), so push them to **7.5** — they are your buffer against the average, not just a pass.
- Advantage you have: you already learn structured technical material daily. Treat English like a system — measurable inputs, feedback loops, spaced repetition.
- **Book the real test at the start of Month 11.** A paid deadline is the single biggest motivator. Take one *diagnostic* mock in Week 1 so you know your baseline (expect ~4.5–5.5).

## Skill & Time Allocation

| Skill | Why it matters | Time |
|---|---|---|
| **Input** (listening + reading) | Comprehensible input is the engine of everything — vocabulary, grammar, and ear all grow from it | ~35% |
| **Vocabulary** (Anki, daily) | Biggest single lever on your band; 6.5–7 ≈ 5,000–6,000 word families active | ~20% |
| **Speaking** (shadowing + output) | Fluency and pronunciation only grow by talking daily; can't be crammed | ~20% |
| **Writing** (sentence → essay) | Slowest skill to move; needs feedback loops, not just practice | ~20% |
| **Grammar** (targeted, applied) | Studied only to fix errors you actually make — not as a separate hobby | ~5% |

> Grammar is small **on purpose**. You already have the `english grammar in use.md` notes — grammar is a *reference to fix mistakes*, not a subject to grind. Accuracy comes from producing language and correcting it.

## Daily Non-Negotiables (every single day, ~55 min)

- [ ] **Anki: 15–20 new cards + all reviews** (~20 min) — vocabulary, collocations, and your own error cards. Never skip; a 2-day gap doubles the backlog.
- [ ] **Listening + shadowing, 25–30 min** — listen to a short clip, then *shadow* it (repeat aloud 0.5s behind the speaker, copying rhythm and intonation). This trains the ear **and** the mouth at once.
- [ ] **One-minute spoken monologue, recorded** (~5 min) — speak about anything (your day, a bug you fixed) for 60s, record it, listen back once, note one thing to fix. This kills the fear of speaking.

The remaining ~90 min/day rotates by phase and by the weekly rhythm below.

## End-State Goals (map to band descriptors)

### Listening — target 7.5 (**32–34/40**, your buffer for the overall)
- [ ] Understand natural-speed connected speech (British/Aus/US/Canadian accents) without subtitles for a 5-min clip
- [ ] Take dictation of a 3-min clip with <5% word error rate
- [ ] Recognise weak forms, linking, and elision ("gonna", "wanna", "d'you") in real time
- [ ] Consistently score **32+/40** on Cambridge IELTS 15–18 listening tests under timed conditions

### Reading (Academic) — target 7.5 (**33–34/40**, your buffer for the overall)
- [ ] Read a 900-word article and answer detail questions without re-reading the whole text
- [ ] Skim for gist in <2 min; scan for a specific fact in <30s
- [ ] Handle all question types: T/F/NG, matching headings, sentence completion, matching features, MCQ
- [ ] Finish 3 passages / 40 questions in 60 minutes with **33+ correct**
- [ ] **[GT]** GT band scale is stricter — Reading 7.5 needs **~36/40**; drill for near-perfect accuracy on the easier texts

### Writing — target 6.5+ (reliable, not occasional — this is the floor that decides the whole test)
- [ ] **Task 2 (essay):** write a clear 4-paragraph 260-word essay in 40 min with a defensible position, topic sentences, and linked ideas
- [ ] **Task 1 [Academic]:** describe a graph/table/process/map in 150 words in 20 min — overview + key features, no opinion
- [ ] **Task 1 [GT]:** write a 150-word letter (formal/semi-formal/informal) matching the right tone
- [ ] Use a **range** of complex sentences (relative clauses, conditionals, passives) with **more correct than incorrect**
- [ ] Use topic-appropriate vocabulary and collocations, not memorized "big words" misused
- [ ] Self-diagnose your own writing against all four criteria before handing it to a tutor/AI

### Speaking — target 6.5+ (reliable floor)
- [ ] Speak for 1–2 min on any Part 2 cue card without long pauses or drying up
- [ ] Answer Part 3 abstract questions with a reason + example, not one-liners
- [ ] Be understood easily throughout; correct word stress and clear individual sounds
- [ ] Self-correct audibly when you make an error (examiners reward this)
- [ ] Use discourse markers naturally ("the thing is", "having said that", "to be honest")

### Vocabulary & Grammar (underpins all four)
- [ ] ~5,000–6,000 word families recognized; ~3,000 usable in speech/writing
- [ ] Know words as **collocations**, not isolated ("make a decision", "heavy rain", "strong argument")
- [ ] Produce complex sentences correctly: conditionals, relative clauses, passive, perfect aspect
- [ ] Personal error log of your top-20 recurring mistakes, each drilled until gone

## The Four Engines (the method behind every phase)

1. **Input** — you cannot output language you've never absorbed. Listen and read *massively* at a level where you understand ~90–95% (comprehensible input). Below that it's noise; at 100% you learn nothing new.
2. **Vocabulary via spaced repetition** — Anki daily, learning words in context and as collocations. This is non-negotiable and compounds like interest.
3. **Pronunciation from Day 1** — via shadowing. If you learn a word's sound wrong, you'll mishear it forever and mispronounce it in the Speaking test. Fix the ear and mouth early.
4. **Output with feedback** — speaking and writing produced daily, then *corrected*. Practice without correction just fossilizes errors. Feedback = a tutor, a language exchange, or a carefully-prompted AI checker.

## Phase 1: Foundation — Rebuild the Base (Months 1–3, ~225 hrs)

> Goal: reach solid **B1**. Fix pronunciation, lock in core grammar, build the first 2,500 high-frequency words, and get 30+ min of daily listening comfortable. **No full IELTS tests yet** (except the Week-1 diagnostic).

### Diagnostic + Setup (Week 1)
- [ ] Take one full Cambridge IELTS mock (book 15) — record all four band scores as your baseline
- [ ] Install **Anki**; add the pre-made **NGSL (New General Service List, ~2,800 words)** deck
- [ ] Set Anki to 15 new cards/day; commit to daily reviews forever
- [ ] Print/bookmark the **IELTS band descriptors** for Writing and Speaking — you'll self-grade against them all year
- [ ] Set up an **error log** file (`english/error-log.md`) — every correction goes here

### Pronunciation Bootcamp (Weeks 1–4, daily 15 min)
- [ ] Learn the **44 English phonemes** (IPA) — use the *BBC Learning English "The Sounds of English"* videos (44 short clips)
- [ ] Drill **minimal pairs** you can't hear/say (ship/sheep, bad/bed, rice/lice, three/tree) with an app (English File Pronunciation, or Sounds: Pronunciation App)
- [ ] Learn **word stress** rules — every multi-syllable word has one strong syllable (PHOtograph → phoTOgraphy). Mark stress on every new Anki word.
- [ ] Learn **connected speech**: linking, weak forms (schwa /ə/), contractions — why "want to" sounds like "wanna"
- [ ] Start **shadowing** 5 min/day using clear, slow content (BBC 6 Minute English)
- [ ] **Exit check:** you can produce the vowel/consonant sounds that don't exist in your L1, and you always know where the stress falls on a new word

### Core Grammar Repair (Weeks 1–8, Fridays + a bit daily, ~40 hrs)
> Use your own `english grammar in use.md` notes + the *English Grammar in Use (Murphy, blue/intermediate)* book. One unit at a time.
- [ ] Lock the **tense system** cold: present simple vs continuous, past simple vs present perfect, past perfect, future forms — do the Murphy exercises until you get them right without thinking
- [ ] Articles (a/an/the/zero) — the #1 error for most learners; do targeted drills
- [ ] Subject–verb agreement, plurals, countable/uncountable + quantifiers (much/many/some/any)
- [ ] Prepositions of time/place, and dependent prepositions (interested **in**, good **at**, depend **on**)
- [ ] Question formation and word order (you already have this in your notes — drill until automatic)
- [ ] Build **complex sentences**: relative clauses, `because/although/while`, first & second conditionals
- [ ] **Exit check:** you can write 10 sentences about your day using 5 different tenses with correct articles and agreement

### Vocabulary Build (Weeks 1–12, daily via Anki, ~35 hrs)
- [ ] Finish the **NGSL 2,800** deck (recognition) — ~2,800 words at 15/day covers the phase
- [ ] For every word, learn **one collocation and its stress** — not the bare word
- [ ] Add a **"mine your input"** habit: every day, pull 5–10 unknown words from what you read/heard into Anki *in the sentence you found them*
- [ ] **Exit check:** you recognize ~2,500 of the most frequent English words and understand ~90% of graded/B1 material

### Massive Input (Weeks 1–12, daily, the core of the phase)
- [ ] **Listening:** BBC 6 Minute English, *every* episode; then graded podcasts (Luke's English Podcast for beginners, "Culips", "The English We Speak"). 30 min/day.
- [ ] **Shadowing:** pick one 2–3 min segment/week and shadow it until you can match the rhythm
- [ ] **Reading:** **graded readers** (Oxford Bookworms / Penguin Readers) — start at your level (A2–B1) and read *whole books* for pleasure and volume, not study. 1–2 books/week.
- [ ] **Dictation** (Wed): transcribe a 2–3 min clip, then compare to transcript, add every missed word to Anki
- [ ] **Exit check:** you can watch BBC 6 Minute English and follow it without the transcript

### Output Starts Small (Weeks 5–12, daily)
- [ ] Daily 1-min recorded monologue (from the non-negotiables) — by Week 12 aim for 2 min without freezing
- [ ] Weekly: write a **5–7 sentence paragraph** about a familiar topic; self-check against the error log
- [ ] Start a **journal in English** — 3 sentences/day minimum; it's low-pressure output volume

**Phase 1 Gate**
- [ ] Understand BBC 6 Minute English with no transcript
- [ ] Write a coherent 100-word paragraph with correct tenses and articles (mostly)
- [ ] Speak 2 minutes on a familiar topic without drying up
- [ ] ~2,500 word families recognized; Anki habit unbroken for 12 weeks
- [ ] Re-take a listening test: aim 20+/40 (up from baseline)

## Phase 2: Bridge to B2 (Months 4–6, ~225 hrs)

> Goal: reach **B2**. Push vocabulary into the academic range, make complex grammar productive (not just recognized), move from graded to authentic material, and turn output into structured paragraphs. Still mostly skill-building; begin *light* exposure to IELTS formats at the end.

### Vocabulary: Academic + Depth (daily)
- [ ] Add the **Academic Word List (AWL, 570 word families)** deck — the vocabulary IELTS Reading/Writing rewards
- [ ] Add a **topic-vocabulary** habit: IELTS recycles ~12 themes (environment, education, technology, health, work, crime, urbanisation, globalisation, media, tourism, culture, government). Build a mini-deck per theme with collocations.
- [ ] Learn words in **word families** (analyse / analysis / analytical / analyst) — needed for Writing lexical range
- [ ] **Exit check:** ~4,000 word families recognized; you can produce topic collocations on the 12 IELTS themes

### Grammar Becomes Productive (weekly)
- [ ] Move to *English Grammar in Use (intermediate)* harder units: passives, reported speech, all conditionals, relative clauses, `-ing` vs `to`-infinitive (you have notes on these already)
- [ ] Drill until you **produce** them in writing under time, not just recognize them
- [ ] Weekly: rewrite one of your old paragraphs using 3 complex structures you were avoiding
- [ ] **Exit check:** your writing naturally contains relative clauses, conditionals, and passives — correctly more often than not

### Input Goes Authentic (daily)
- [ ] **Listening:** graduate to natural-speed content — TED Talks (with then without subtitles), BBC News, *All Ears English* / *IELTS Energy* podcasts, YouTube channels in your field (English is a bonus: engineering talks)
- [ ] Add **accent variety** deliberately: British, American, Australian, Canadian — the test uses all
- [ ] **Reading:** graduate from graded readers to authentic B1–B2 material — *BBC News*, *Simple Wikipedia → Wikipedia*, *Aeon*, *The Conversation*, popular-science articles. Also read tech articles you'd read anyway, but *mine vocabulary* from them.
- [ ] Keep **intensive dictation** weekly; word-error-rate should be falling
- [ ] **Exit check:** you follow a TED talk without subtitles and get the main points + most details

### Speaking Ramps Up (daily + weekly)
- [ ] Continue daily recorded monologues, now 2–3 min on **abstract** topics (opinions, not just facts)
- [ ] Begin **weekly live speaking** — get a tutor on **iTalki / Preply** (cheap, ~1×/week) or a free language-exchange partner (Tandem/HelloTalk). Nothing replaces real conversation.
- [ ] Learn **fluency fillers & discourse markers** so you stop pausing silently ("well", "I mean", "the thing is", "as far as I know")
- [ ] Shadow to fix pronunciation of your **top mispronounced words** (from tutor feedback)
- [ ] **Exit check:** you hold a 10-min conversation with a tutor on an everyday topic without breaking down

### Writing Becomes Structured (weekly)
- [ ] Learn **paragraph architecture**: topic sentence → explanation → example → link. Write one every week.
- [ ] Learn **cohesion**: linking words (however, moreover, in contrast, as a result) used *correctly and sparingly* — overuse is penalized
- [ ] Introduce the **IELTS Writing Task 2 essay shape** (intro → 2 body paragraphs → conclusion) but only after paragraphs are solid — practice 1 short essay/week
- [ ] Get **every piece corrected** (tutor or a well-prompted AI: ask it to grade against the four IELTS criteria and list specific errors, not rewrite for you)
- [ ] Move every correction into the **error log** and Anki
- [ ] **Exit check:** you write a clear, linked 200-word opinion paragraph-set that a tutor rates ~6

**Phase 2 Gate**
- [ ] Follow authentic natural-speed audio (TED/news) without subtitles
- [ ] ~4,000 word families; producing academic collocations on IELTS themes
- [ ] Hold a 10-minute conversation with a tutor
- [ ] Write a coherent 200-word structured argument, corrected and logged
- [ ] Listening mock 24+/40; Reading mock 22+/40

## Phase 3: IELTS Skills — Aim the Language at the Exam (Months 7–9, ~225 hrs)

> Goal: learn every IELTS **task type and strategy**, and start scoring near target on individual sections. Now you know enough English to make "IELTS technique" actually pay off.

### Listening Strategy (weekly intensive + daily input)
- [ ] Learn each section's shape: S1 (social, form-filling), S2 (monologue, map/labels), S3 (academic discussion, 2–4 speakers), S4 (lecture, note completion)
- [ ] Master question types: form/note/table completion, multiple choice, matching, map/plan labeling, sentence completion
- [ ] Train the **"read ahead + predict"** skill: use the pre-audio time to predict answer type (number? name? noun?)
- [ ] Drill **spelling + numbers** — you lose easy marks on misspelled correct answers; British spelling
- [ ] Watch for **distractors** (the speaker says a wrong option then corrects it) — the exam's favorite trap
- [ ] Do 2 section-drills + 1 full listening test/week from Cambridge IELTS 15–18
- [ ] **Exit check:** consistent 28+/40

### Reading Strategy (weekly)
- [ ] Learn the **question-type playbook**:
  - [ ] **True/False/Not Given** — the highest-error type. NG = *not stated*, False = *contradicted*. Drill the distinction until reliable.
  - [ ] **Matching Headings** — read topic sentences, summarize each paragraph in 3 words first
  - [ ] **Sentence/summary completion** — scan for paraphrase, watch word limits
  - [ ] **Matching features / information to paragraphs** — the time sink; do these last
  - [ ] **MCQ** — eliminate wrong options
- [ ] Learn **skim then scan** timing: ~2 min skim per passage, then question-by-question scanning; **20 min per passage max**
- [ ] Train **paraphrase recognition** — the answer is never the same words as the question; this is the whole skill
- [ ] Do 3 passages timed, then *review every wrong answer* and write *why* it was wrong (log the trap type)
- [ ] **Exit check:** consistent 28+/40 in 60 min

### Writing Task 1 (weekly)
- [ ] **[Academic]** Learn all chart types: line, bar, pie, table, process diagram, map, multiple/mixed charts
  - [ ] Structure: intro (paraphrase the prompt) → **overview** (2–3 biggest trends, *no numbers*) → 2 detail paragraphs with data
  - [ ] Learn **describing-change language**: rose/fell/plateaued/surged, sharp/gradual, doubled/halved; and comparison language
  - [ ] The **overview paragraph is mandatory** — missing it caps you at band 5
- [ ] **[GT]** Learn the 3 letter registers (formal/semi-formal/informal), the standard openings/closings, and the 3 bullet points must all be covered
- [ ] Write 2 Task 1s/week, corrected against band descriptors, timed at 20 min
- [ ] **Exit check:** Task 1 rated ~6.5 by tutor; written in 20 min

### Writing Task 2 (weekly — the highest-value skill)
- [ ] Learn the **5 essay types** and a flexible plan for each:
  - [ ] **Opinion** (to what extent do you agree/disagree)
  - [ ] **Discussion** (discuss both views + give your opinion)
  - [ ] **Problem–Solution** (problems and solutions / causes and solutions)
  - [ ] **Advantages–Disadvantages**
  - [ ] **Two-part / direct question**
- [ ] Master the machine that scores well: **clear position in the intro**, one central idea per body paragraph, **developed** with explanation + example, real conclusion
- [ ] Learn to **plan for 5 min** before writing — unplanned essays lose Coherence marks
- [ ] Learn **paraphrasing the question** (don't copy it) and precise **topic vocabulary**
- [ ] Kill the band-killers: memorized template phrases misused, off-topic drift, no clear position, list-of-ideas with no development
- [ ] Write 2 essays/week (40 min each), corrected against **all four criteria**, errors logged
- [ ] **Exit check:** Task 2 rated ~6.5 by an independent grader; written in 40 min

### Speaking — All Three Parts (weekly tutor + daily drills)
- [ ] **Part 1** (4–5 min, familiar topics): give a reason + a small detail, never one-word answers. Prepare the common topics (work, hometown, hobbies, food, weather).
- [ ] **Part 2** (cue card, 1-min prep, 1–2 min talk): learn a flexible structure (what/when/where → detail → why it matters → feeling). Practice from a big bank of cue cards.
- [ ] **Part 3** (abstract discussion): answer with **opinion → reason → example → (concession)**; learn to speculate and compare
- [ ] Build **paraphrase + idiomatic range** (band 7 needs "less common" vocabulary used naturally)
- [ ] Drill **fluency** — the goal is speaking *without long pauses*, even if imperfect; self-correction is fine and rewarded
- [ ] Do a **weekly mock speaking test** with your tutor scoring you on the 4 criteria
- [ ] Record and re-listen to find recurring pronunciation/grammar errors → Anki
- [ ] **Exit check:** tutor rates you ~6.5 across all three parts

**Phase 3 Gate**
- [ ] Listening 30+/40, Reading 30+/40 consistently (heading for the **32–34** band-7.5 buffer)
- [ ] Task 1 and Task 2 both rated **at least** 6.5, written under time — 6.5 must be the floor, not the ceiling
- [ ] Speaking rated **at least** 6.5 by tutor across all parts
- [ ] No section lagging below 6.5 — if one is, it owns most of Phase 4's time
- [ ] You can name the strategy for every question type without notes

## Phase 4: Exam Mastery — Timing, Mocks, Weakness Killing (Months 10–12, ~225 hrs)

> Goal: convert skill into **exam-day performance**. Full timed mocks, ruthless weakness targeting, stamina, and test-day logistics. **Book the real test for late Month 11 / Month 12.**

### Full Mock Test Cycle (weekly)
- [ ] Do **1 full timed mock/week** (Listening + Reading + Writing back-to-back, then Speaking with tutor) from Cambridge IELTS 16–19 (newest books = closest to current test)
- [ ] Simulate real conditions: no pausing audio, no dictionary, strict timing, handwritten if paper-based (or on-screen if computer-delivered — **decide your format now** and practice it)
- [ ] After each mock: **error autopsy** — categorize every lost mark by cause, and the next week's drills target the top category
- [ ] Build **stamina** — the test is ~2h40m of Listening+Reading+Writing with no breaks; the last essay is where tired candidates lose the band

### Weakness Targeting (data-driven, weekly)
- [ ] Keep a **mock scorecard** table: date, L / R / W / S bands, top error category — watch the trend line
- [ ] Whichever section is furthest from target gets **60% of the rotating time** that week
- [ ] Common late-stage fixes:
  - [ ] Reading: still slow → drill scanning + skip-and-return; still missing T/F/NG → dedicated NG drills
  - [ ] Listening: still misspelling → spelling drills; missing S3/S4 → academic-lecture note practice
  - [ ] Writing: stuck at 6 → almost always **task response** (not answering fully) or **repetitive grammar** — get 5 essays expert-graded to find the ceiling
  - [ ] Speaking: stuck at 6 → usually **fluency** (too many pauses) or **narrow vocabulary** — more talking, more idiom

### Writing/Speaking Polish (weekly, with feedback)
- [ ] Get **at least 5 essays professionally graded** (a real IELTS examiner service, or an experienced iTalki teacher) — AI is fine for volume but can't reliably band-score you at the 6/7 boundary
- [ ] Assemble a **personal bank** of flexible phrases for cohesion, opinion, and paraphrase — internalized, *not* memorized templates (examiners penalize obvious templates)
- [ ] Speaking: 2 full mock interviews/week; eliminate your top-3 recurring errors
- [ ] Refine **pronunciation** on the words you actually stumble on under pressure

### Test-Day Readiness (Month 12)
- [ ] Do your **final 3 mocks under exact real conditions** (same time of day as your test slot)
- [ ] Memorize the **timing plan** per section (e.g., Reading: 20/20/20 min per passage; Writing: 20 min Task 1, 40 min Task 2, spend the extra on Task 2 which is worth double)
- [ ] Know the **logistics**: ID, arrival time, computer vs paper interface, Speaking may be a different day/video call
- [ ] Learn to **not panic** on a hard listening section — miss one, move on; don't lose the next three
- [ ] Sleep, don't cram the day before

**Phase 4 / Exam Gate**
- [ ] 3 consecutive full mocks at **overall 7.0 with no band below 6.5** — the full success condition, not just a good average
- [ ] Listening & Reading landing **7.5 (32–34/40)** to carry the average — the L7.5·R7.5·W6.5·S6.5 = 7.0 plan
- [ ] Writing consistently ≥6.5 from an **independent** grader (not self-assessed) — this is the make-or-break floor
- [ ] Speaking consistently ≥6.5 from a tutor
- [ ] **No single mock section under 6.5** in your last three attempts
- [ ] Timing is automatic; you finish every section with margin
- [ ] **Booked and sat the real test**

## Vocabulary System (the compounding engine)

> This is 20% of your time but drives your whole band. Treat Anki like your daily standup — non-negotiable.

- **Volume:** 15–20 new cards/day × ~300 days ≈ 4,500–6,000 items. That is the difference between a 5 and a 7.
- **Card design:** front = word *in a real sentence* with a blank; back = meaning + collocation + stress mark + example. Never learn a bare word→translation pair.
- **Learn collocations, not words:** native-like word choice ("commit a crime", "heavy traffic", "make progress", "a strong argument") is exactly what "Lexical Resource" band 7 rewards.
- **Mine your input:** the best words are the ones you meet in your own reading/listening — they come with context and stick.
- **Word families:** learn all forms (economy / economic / economical / economically) — needed to avoid repetition in Writing.
- **Decks in order:** NGSL 2,800 (Phase 1) → AWL 570 (Phase 2) → IELTS-topic collocation decks (Phase 2–3) → your personal error/mining deck (all phases).
- **Active recall:** once a week, try to *use* 10 recent words in written sentences — recognition ≠ production.

## Pronunciation Guide (start Day 1, never stop)

> You can't hear a sound you can't produce, and you can't produce a sound you can't hear — so train both together via shadowing. Fixing this early prevents a fossilized accent that caps Speaking.

- **44 phonemes (IPA):** learn the full chart; focus on sounds absent from your first language.
- **Minimal pairs:** drill the contrasts you personally confuse until you can both hear and say them.
- **Word stress:** every new word gets its stressed syllable marked in Anki. Wrong stress makes correct words unintelligible.
- **Sentence stress & rhythm:** English is stress-timed — content words are stressed, function words reduced to schwa. This is what makes you sound fluent.
- **Connected speech:** linking ("an_apple"), weak forms ("was" → /wəz/), elision, assimilation ("gonna", "d'you"). Understanding this is half of Listening.
- **Intonation:** rising for yes/no questions and lists, falling for statements and Wh-questions.
- **Shadowing method:** (1) listen to a short clip, (2) read the transcript aloud with it, (3) shadow 0.5s behind copying rhythm, (4) record yourself, (5) compare. 15–20 min/day is transformative over a year.

## Writing Task Bank

### Task 2 essay types & flexible plans
| Type | Prompt signal | Body plan |
|---|---|---|
| Opinion | "To what extent do you agree/disagree" | State position → 2 paragraphs supporting it (or 1 support + 1 concession) |
| Discussion | "Discuss both views and give your opinion" | Para 1 = view A, Para 2 = view B, make **your** opinion clear throughout + conclusion |
| Problem–Solution | "What problems… what solutions" | Para 1 = problems (with effects), Para 2 = solutions (with how they work) |
| Advantages–Disadvantages | "Do the advantages outweigh…" | Para 1 = advantages, Para 2 = disadvantages, **weigh them** and conclude |
| Two-part question | Two direct questions | One paragraph answering each, both fully developed |

- **Every body paragraph:** topic sentence → explain the idea → concrete example → link back to the question.
- **Intro:** paraphrase the prompt + state your position/roadmap. **Conclusion:** restate position, no new ideas.
- **The four scored criteria (equal weight):** Task Response · Coherence & Cohesion · Lexical Resource · Grammatical Range & Accuracy.

### Task 1 [Academic] language kit
- **Trend verbs:** rise/increase/climb/surge, fall/decline/drop/plummet, level off/plateau/remain stable, fluctuate, peak at, dip to.
- **Degree:** sharply/dramatically/steeply vs gradually/slightly/marginally.
- **Structure:** paraphrase prompt → **overview (biggest trends, no data)** → 2 detail paragraphs. Overview is mandatory.

## Band Descriptor Bank — "What examiners reward / what fails"

> Answer these about your *own* writing and speaking. This is the equivalent of the interview depth bank: know exactly why marks are won and lost.

### Writing — why a 6 stays a 6
- **Task Response — the most common ceiling.** A 6 often "addresses the task" but doesn't *fully* answer it, or has a position that isn't clear/consistent, or ideas that are stated but not *developed* (no explanation/example). A 7 answers every part, holds a clear position, and develops each idea. **Fix:** plan for 5 min, one idea per paragraph, always explain *and* exemplify.
- **Coherence & Cohesion.** A 6 either underuses linking (choppy) or *overuses* it (mechanical "Firstly/Secondly/Moreover" on every sentence — this is penalized). A 7 has logical flow and referencing (this/such/these) with linking used naturally. **Fix:** one clear topic sentence per paragraph; vary and reduce connectors.
- **Lexical Resource.** A 6 repeats common words and misuses "big" words. A 7 uses **less common vocabulary and collocations** appropriately, with only occasional errors. **Fix:** topic collocations, not thesaurus-dumping.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy.** A 6 writes mostly simple sentences, or complex ones with frequent errors. A 7 has a **range** of complex structures and **more error-free sentences than not**. **Fix:** deliberately deploy relative clauses, conditionals, passives — correctly.

### Speaking — why a 6 stays a 6
- **Fluency & Coherence.** A 6 has noticeable pausing/self-correction that interrupts flow. A 7 speaks at length with only occasional hesitation and uses discourse markers. **Fix:** the goal is *keep talking* — fillers and self-correction beat silence.
- **Lexical Resource.** A 6 has enough words but limited flexibility. A 7 paraphrases, uses idiomatic language and less common items. **Fix:** learn topic collocations and 2–3 idioms per theme, used naturally.
- **Grammatical Range & Accuracy.** Same as writing — range of structures, more accuracy. **Fix:** use complex sentences aloud in daily monologues until automatic.
- **Pronunciation.** A 6 is understandable but with lapses; L1 accent affects clarity. A 7 is easy to understand throughout with good stress/intonation. **Fix:** word stress + connected speech from the pronunciation bootcamp; accent is fine, *unclear sounds* are not.

### Listening — where marks leak
- **Spelling/number errors** on otherwise-correct answers — British spelling, careful with numbers/dates.
- **Distractors** — the speaker mentions a wrong option, then corrects it; the last thing said is usually the answer.
- **Losing your place** after one hard question and missing the next three. **Fix:** if you miss one, let it go instantly.

### Reading — where marks leak
- **True/False/Not Given confusion** — *Not Given* means the text neither states nor contradicts it; *False* means it contradicts. Don't use outside knowledge.
- **Word-limit violations** — "no more than two words" and you write three = zero, even if right.
- **Running out of time** on passage 3 because passage 1 was over-read. **Fix:** hard 20-min cap per passage.

## One-Screen Summary

| Phase | Months | Focus | Exit signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 Foundation | 1–3 | Pronunciation, core grammar, NGSL 2,800, massive graded input | Follow 6 Minute English; 2-min monologue; ~2,500 words |
| 2 Bridge to B2 | 4–6 | AWL + topic vocab, productive complex grammar, authentic input, tutor speaking, structured writing | Follow TED w/o subs; 10-min convo; 200-word argument; ~4,000 words |
| 3 IELTS Skills | 7–9 | Every task type + strategy; sections near target | L/R 32+/40 (band 7.5); Task 1&2 ≥6.5; Speaking ≥6.5 |
| 4 Exam Mastery | 10–12 | Full timed mocks, weakness killing, stamina, test day | 3 mocks at overall 7.0, no band <6.5; test booked and sat |

> **The whole plan in one line:** build the language for six months (input + vocab + pronunciation + output-with-feedback, every day), then aim it at the exam for six months (task types + timed mocks + relentless weakness targeting) — and never skip the daily Anki + listening + speaking.
>
> **The scoring rule that governs everything:** overall 7.0 = average rounded to nearest 0.5, and *no band may drop below 6.5*. The safe formula is **L7.5 · R7.5 · W6.5 · S6.5 = exactly 7.0** — make Listening and Reading (the controllable ones) your buffer, and never let Writing or Speaking slip under 6.5.
