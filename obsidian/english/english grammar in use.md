# English Grammar in Use

> Notation: `V` = base verb, `V-ing` = present participle, `V-ed` = past simple, `V-pp` = past participle, `S` = subject.

## Tenses

### Present continuous — `S + am/are/is + V-ing`
- happening now, or around now (today, this week, this year, ...)
- temporary situations (not permanent) — "I'm living with friends until I find a flat"
- a change that has started to happen
	- getting, becoming
	- changing, improving
	- starting, beginning
	- increasing, rising, falling, growing
- `always + V-ing` → complaint / annoyance ("You're always losing your keys")
- `am/are/is + being` → behaviour now, not permanent character ("He's being rude" vs "He is rude")

### Present simple — `S + V(-s)`
- facts, permanent situations, habits, repeated actions
- 3rd person singular adds `-s/-es`; use `do/does` for negatives and questions
- position of frequency adverbs (always, usually, often, sometimes, never): before the main verb, after `be`
- these **state verbs** are normally not used in continuous form:
	- apologize, advise, insist, agree, refuse, suggest
	- like, want, need, prefer
	- know, realize, understand, recognize
	- believe, suppose, remember, mean
	- belong, fit, contain, consist, seem
	- think (= consider) · but "I'm thinking" (= considering) is fine
	- see, hear, smell, taste, look, feel

### Present perfect — `S + have/has + V-pp`
- how many / how much? → a completed action with a **present result**
- a state or repeated action continuing up to now (with `for/since`) — "I've known her for years"
- markers: just (a short time ago), already (before expected), yet (until now — negative/question)
- also: ever, never, recently, lately, so far, up to now, in the last few days
- superlatives & "first time" take present perfect — "It's the best film I've ever seen", "This is the first time I've driven here"
- `have/has been to` = visited and come back ; `have/has gone to` = went and is still there
- do **not** use with finished past time: yesterday, last year, when I was young, ...
	- (American English often uses past simple with just/already/yet)

### Present perfect continuous — `S + have/has been + V-ing`
- how long? → an activity that has been happening (may still be going, or just stopped)
- use `for` (period) / `since` (starting point) to say how long
- emphasizes the **activity/duration**; present perfect simple emphasizes the **result/completion**
	- "I've been reading this book" (activity) vs "I've read this book" (finished)

### Past simple — `S + V-ed`
- finished action at a definite past time; chronological order
- regular verbs `+ -ed`; many common verbs are irregular
- `did` for negatives and questions ("Did you go?", "I didn't go")

### Past continuous — `S + was/were + V-ing`
- a longer background action in progress in the past
- something happened (past simple) in the middle of something else (past continuous)
- two long actions at the same time — "While I was cooking, she was reading"

### Past perfect — `S + had + V-pp`
- an action completed before another past action (an "earlier past")
- makes non-linear order clear — "The train had left when we arrived"
- common in reported speech and after `when/because/after`

### Past perfect continuous — `S + had been + V-ing`
- how long an activity had been going on up to a point in the past
- "He was tired because he had been working all day"

### have / have got
- for possession, relationship, illness, appointment — not for actions/experiences
- `have got` is more informal, mainly present tense
- for actions we **do** use `have` (not `have got`): have breakfast, have a shower, have a look, have a good time — these **can** be continuous ("I'm having lunch")

### used to — `S + used to + V`
- I did it often / it was true in the past, but not any more
- past only — there is **no** present form (use present simple instead)
- negative/question: `didn't use to` / `did you use to ...?`
- ≠ `be used to` (§ used to, below) — different meaning

### if / when (future time clauses)
- `when/while/before/after/as soon as/until + present simple` = future time (don't use `will` in the clause)
- `when + present perfect` = one thing completes before the other — "I'll call when I've finished"
- `if` = uncertain; `when` = certain it will happen

## The future

### Present tenses for the future
- **present continuous** → a fixed arrangement (time/place decided) — "I'm meeting Tom at 8"
- **present simple** → timetables, programmes, schedules — "The train leaves at 9"

### going to — `S + (be) going to + V`
- a prior intention / plan (arranged or not)
- a prediction based on present evidence — "Look at those clouds — it's going to rain"

### will / shall — `S + will/shall + V`
- a decision made at the moment of speaking; offering, agreeing, promising, refusing
- predictions/opinions about the future (think, expect, probably, I'm sure)
- **will vs going to**: `going to` = already decided; `will` = decided now
- `shall I / shall we ...?` for offers and suggestions

### Future continuous — `S + will be + V-ing`
- I will be in the middle of an action at a future time
- also for something that will happen in the normal course of events — "I'll be seeing her tomorrow"

### Future perfect — `S + will have + V-pp`
- it will already be complete before a time in the future — "By 2030 they will have finished"

## Modal verbs

| Modal | Epistemic (probability) | Deontic (obligation / permission) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **must** | logical deduction (_"It must be raining."_) | absolute obligation (_"You must stop."_) |
| **can** | general possibility (_"It can get cold here."_) | permission / ability (_"Can I go?"_ / _"I can drive."_) |
| **could** | weak possibility (_"It could rain."_) | past ability / polite request (_"Could you help?"_) |
| **may** | factual possibility (_"He may accept the job."_) | formal permission (_"May I enter?"_) |
| **might** | remote possibility (_"They might lose."_) | very formal permission (_"Might I suggest ...?"_) |
| **should** | expectation (_"They should be here soon."_) | advice / weak obligation (_"You should eat healthier."_) |
| **will** | certain prediction (_"The sun will rise."_) | volition / intent (_"I will help you."_) |
| **would** | hypothetical probability (_"That would be difficult."_) | past habit / conditional (_"I would go if I had time."_) |

- modals take the bare infinitive (no `to`) and don't change form (no `-s`, no `did`)
- past form = `modal + have + V-pp` (must have, could have, should have, ...)

### can / could / (be) able to
- something is possible or allowed; somebody has the ability to do it
- `can` has no future/infinitive form → use `be able to` ("I'll be able to", "I'd like to be able to")
- `could` (general past ability) ≠ `was/were able to` (= managed to, on a specific occasion)
- `could have done` = it was possible but didn't happen

### must / can't (deduction)
- `must` = I'm sure it's true ; `can't` = I'm sure it's not true
- past: `must have done` / `can't have done`

### may / might
- something is possible (perhaps ~50/50)
- `may be` (verb) ≠ `maybe` (adverb = perhaps)
- `could` ≈ `may/might`; but `couldn't` (impossible) is stronger than `may/might not` (perhaps not)
- use `might` (not `may`) when the situation is imaginary/unreal
- `may/might as well do` = do it because there's no better alternative

### have to / must / need
- `must` (speaker's own authority/feeling) vs `have to` (external rules/facts); `have to` is more common in questions & past
- `mustn't` = it is forbidden (don't do it)
- `don't have to` = `needn't` = not necessary (but allowed) — note the different meaning from `mustn't`
- `needn't have done` = did it, but it wasn't necessary ; `didn't need to do` = wasn't necessary (may or may not have done it)

### should / ought to / had better
- `should` = `ought to` = the right/sensible thing to do (advice, opinion, expectation)
- `should have done` = didn't do the right thing ; `shouldn't have done` = did something wrong
- use `should` after:
	- insist, demand, recommend, suggest, propose (that ...)
	- adjectives: strange, odd, funny, typical, natural, interesting, surprised, surprising
- `had better (do)` = advisable **for a specific situation now**, or there'll be a problem — stronger/more urgent than `should`

### would
- imagining a situation/action (not real); `would have done` for the unreal past
- past repeated actions/habits (like `used to`, but only for actions) — "When we were kids, we would play outside"
- `would you mind ...?`, `I would think/imagine ...` (polite/tentative)

### it's time
- `it's time (for somebody) to do` — "It's time to go"
- `it's time somebody did` (past form, present meaning) — "It's time you went home"

## Conditional sentences

### Zero conditional — general truth / always true
- `if S + V, S + V` — "If you heat ice, it melts"

### 1st conditional — real / likely future
- `if S + V, S will + V`
- something likely to happen; a decision — "If it rains, we'll stay in"
- `will` can be replaced by can/may/might/should/imperative

### 2nd conditional — unreal / unlikely present or future
- `if S + V-ed, S would/could/might + V`
- something we don't expect to happen, or imaginary — "If I won the lottery, I'd travel"
- use `were` for all persons in careful/formal English — "If I were you ..."

### 3rd conditional — unreal past
- `if S + had + V-pp, S would/could/might have + V-pp`
- something that didn't happen in the past — "If I had known, I would have called"

### Mixed conditional
- past condition → present result: `if S + had + V-pp, S would + V` — "If I had taken the job, I'd be rich now"

### unless / other links
- `unless` = if ... not — "I won't go unless you come"
- also: as long as, provided/providing (that), in case, otherwise, even if

### wish / if only
- `wish/if only + V-ed` → regret about the **present** — "I wish I had a car"
- `wish/if only + had + V-pp` → regret about the **past** — "I wish I had studied"
- `wish/if only + would` → complaint / wanting something/somebody to change — "I wish it would stop raining"
- `I wish I could do` = I regret I can't ; `I wish I could have done` = I regret I couldn't
- `wish` vs `hope`: `wish` = unreal/regret ; `hope + present/will` = a real future possibility ("I hope it doesn't rain")

## Passive
- `be + V-pp` — the object of the active becomes the subject; use it when the doer is unknown, obvious, or unimportant
- name the doer with `by` only when it adds information — "It was written by Orwell"
- forms in every tense: is done / is being done / was done / has been done / will be done / can be done / to be done / being done
- verbs with two objects can passivize either — "I was given a book" / "A book was given to me"
- `get` passive for things that happen (often unexpected/informal) — "got broken", "got stolen"
- reporting-verb passives:
	- `It is said/believed/thought that ...`
	- `S + is said/believed/thought + to (do/have done)` — "He is said to be rich"

## Reported speech
- `say` (no person, or `say to somebody`) vs `tell` (`tell somebody`) — "He said (that) ..." / "He told me (that) ..."
- **backshift** (main verb one tense back): am/is→was, do→did, have done→had done, will→would, can→could, must→had to
	- if the situation is still true, backshift is optional
- pronouns, and time/place words shift: now→then, today→that day, tomorrow→the next day, here→there, this→that
- reported **questions** keep statement word order, no `do`, no `?` — "She asked where I lived", "He asked if/whether I was ready"
- reported **commands/requests**: `tell/ask + somebody + to (do)` — "She told me to wait", "He asked me not to go"
- other reporting verbs: `offer/promise/agree/refuse + to`, `suggest/admit + V-ing`, `apologise for`, `insist that`
- `supposed to` = intended / arranged / expected (but different from reality) — "You're supposed to be working"
- `have/get + something + V-pp` = arrange for somebody to do it for you — "I had my car repaired"

## Questions
- statement `S + V` → question `(aux/be) + S + V`; add `do/does/did` when there's no auxiliary
- **subject questions** (who/what/which asks about the subject): no `do`, normal word order — "Who broke the window?"
- in Wh-questions, prepositions usually go at the end — "Where are you from?", "What are you looking at?"
- negative questions:
	- show surprise — "Didn't you know?"
	- suggestions with "Why don't ...?" — "Why don't we eat out tonight?"
	- answering: "Don't you want to go?" → "Yes (I want to)" / "No (I don't)"
- **indirect / embedded questions** keep statement word order, no `do` — "Do you know where Tom has gone?" (not _where has Tom gone_)

## Auxiliary verbs
- used to avoid repeating the main verb, to deny, or to show interest/surprise — "I haven't finished." "Oh, haven't you?"
- **so / neither (nor)** for agreeing: `so + aux + S` (positive) / `neither + aux + S` (negative)
	- "I'm tired." — "So am I." / "I can't swim." — "Neither can I."
- **question tags**: `positive statement, negative tag` ; `negative statement, positive tag`
	- "It's cold, isn't it?" / "You don't like it, do you?"
	- `I am` → tag `aren't I?` ; imperative → `will you?` ; "Let's" → `shall we?`
	- falling intonation = expecting agreement; rising = a real question

## Verb + V-ing / to-infinitive

### Verb + V-ing
- stop, recommend, avoid, imagine, finish, consider, risk, fancy, enjoy, deny, admit, suggest, mind, postpone, miss, practise
- give up, put off, go on, carry on, keep (on)
- `S + V + having done` → to stress a finished action ("He admitted having stolen it")

### Verb + to-infinitive
- offer, plan, manage, deserve, agree, arrange, fail, afford, refuse, hope, promise, learn, decide, forget, threaten, tend, expect, want, would like
- seem, appear, pretend, claim

### Verb + question word + to-infinitive
- ask, know, decide, remember, forget, learn, explain, understand, wonder — "We asked how to get to the station"
- show, tell, ask, advise, teach + somebody + what/how/where to do

### Verb + object + to-infinitive
- tell, advise, remind, warn, invite, encourage, persuade, get, force, teach, allow, enable + somebody + to ...
- `make / let + somebody + V` (no `to`) — but passive `be made to do` takes `to`
- `help` takes `to`-infinitive **or** bare infinitive ("helped me (to) move")

### V-ing vs to-infinitive — meaning changes
- **remember / forget** — `-ing`: recall a past action · `to`: remember/forget to do it (before doing)
- **regret** — `-ing`: sorry about a past action · `to`: sorry to say (bad news) now
- **go on** — `-ing`: continue the same thing · `to`: move on to something new
- **try** — `-ing`: do it as an experiment/test · `to`: make an effort/attempt (something difficult)
- **stop** — `-ing`: end the activity · `to`: pause in order to do something
- **mean** — `-ing`: involve/result in · `to`: intend to
- **need** — `to do` (active) · `-ing` = passive meaning ("The car needs washing" = needs to be washed)

### V-ing vs to-infinitive — little/no meaning change
- begin, start, continue, intend, bother, can't bear
- like, love, hate, prefer — use `-ing` for a situation that already exists / enjoyment
- `would like/love/hate/prefer + to`
	- `would like to have done` = I regret I didn't/couldn't do it
	- `would rather + V` (bare) = would prefer to ; `would rather + S + V-ed` for another person ("I'd rather you didn't")

### used to / be used to
- `used to + V` = did it regularly in the past, but no longer
- `be used to + something / V-ing` = accustomed to it; it isn't new/strange — "I'm used to driving here"
- `get used to + V-ing` = become accustomed

### Preposition + V-ing
- any verb after a preposition takes `-ing`
- `V + (object) + preposition + V-ing`:
	- talk about, apologise for, approve of, decide against, dream of, feel like, insist on, look forward to, succeed in, think of/about
	- accuse ... of, congratulate ... on, prevent ... from, stop ... from, suspect ... of, thank ... for, blame ... for
- `there's no point/use/good (in) + V-ing` ; `it's (not) worth + V-ing`
- `have trouble/difficulty/a problem (in) + V-ing`
- `spend/waste (time/money) + V-ing`
- `go + V-ing` for sports/activities (go swimming, go shopping)
- note: `to` is a preposition (→ `-ing`) in: look forward to, be used to, object to, in addition to

### Adjective + to-infinitive
- hard, easy, nice, safe, cheap, exciting, impossible, difficult, good, dangerous, expensive, interesting
- `it's + adjective + of somebody + to`: kind, generous, careless, silly, stupid, inconsiderate, unfair, typical, nice
- `adjective + to (hear/see/know)`: sorry, surprised, glad, pleased, relieved, amazed, sad, disappointed
- `the first/next/last/only + to do` — "She was the first to arrive"
- sure / likely / bound / due to
- **afraid**
	- `afraid to do` = don't want to because it's dangerous / the result could be bad
	- `afraid of something happening` = worried something bad will happen
- **interested**
	- `interested in doing` = would like to do it
	- `interested to do (hear/see/know)` = it was interesting for me
- **sorry**
	- `sorry for/about doing` = apologise for something done
	- `sorry to do/say` = regret something happening now

### see / hear somebody do vs doing
- `saw somebody do` = saw the complete action (start to finish)
- `saw somebody doing` = saw them in the middle of it (incomplete / in progress)
- same pattern with hear, watch, feel, listen to, notice

### -ing and -ed participle clauses
- `-ing` clause: two things at the same time, or one during another — "She sat reading a book"
- `having done` / `after V-ing` when one action comes before another
- `-ed` clause = passive meaning — "The man injured in the accident was taken to hospital"
